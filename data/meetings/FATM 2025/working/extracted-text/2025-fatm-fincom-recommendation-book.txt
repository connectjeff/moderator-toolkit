Natick Finance Committee
April 8, 2026
Greetings to all Town Meeting Members, Taxpayers, and Residents of Natick,
The Natick Finance Committee’s recommendations for articles appearing on the warrant for the
2026 Spring Annual Town Meeting are enclosed. Under the Town’s Charter, the Finance 
Committee is required to hold public hearings on the Town Administrator’s proposed budget 
for each Town agency and “+le a report containing its recommendations for actions on the 
proposed budget” (Natick Home Rule Charter Article 5 Section 6).
Under the Town’s By-Laws, the Finance Committee is also required to “consider all
matters of business included within the articles of any warrant for a Town Meeting,
and shall, after due consideration, report thereon, in print, its recommendation as to
each article” (Natick By-Laws, Chapter 23, Section 4).  
In the Spring of 2026, the Finance Committee met of the following dates: 2/4, 3/10, 3/12, 3/19, 
3/24, 3/26, and 4/7.  The Capital Subcommittee met on 2/17.  The General Government 
Subcommittee met on: 2/19, 2/24, 2/26, 3/3, and 3/4. The DPW Subcommittee met on 3/5. And 
the Education and Learning Subcommittee met on: 3/2, 3/4, 3/11, and 3/26. 
Please visit the Finance Committee’s Town Meeting Member Resource website, which will
be updated throughout Spring Town Meeting for late breaking information, new and 
supplementary information, scheduling, and communication from the Moderator. 
https://sites.google.com/natickma.org/+ncom
The Finance Committee’s Spring Annual Recommendation Book will be available shortly and 
will be mailed to each Town Meeting Member once printed. Town Meeting Members should 
also expect to receive a Town Administrator’s Amended Budget book, and the Natick Public
Schools’ FY2027 Budget book. Taken together, these documents represent the baseline for 
the Spring 2026 Town Meeting discussions, debates, and decisions. 
Our recommendations are respectfully submitted for your consideration.

Dear T own Meeting Members, 
Thank you for your continued service to the T own of Natick and for the time and care you bring to 
this process. Your engagement is essential to the strength of our community. 
The Natick Public Schools FY27 budget requests an appropriation of $100,650,912, as approved 
by both the School Committee and the Finance Committee. This is a level service budget that 
maintains current programs and stafﬁng while addressing known cost increases, including 
contractual obligations, transportation, and specialized services. 
This budget builds on last year’s override, and we are grateful to the community for the support 
that provided stability after multiple years of reductions. The FY27 budget maintains that stability 
and avoids further cuts. 
More than 99% of this budget maintains existing services. Within that, the district has made 
targeted, budget-neutral reallocations of less than one percent of the total budget. These shifts 
are fully offset, with no net impact on the overall budget and a net reduction of 1.0 FTE. 
These adjustments strengthen student support, improve the consistency of instruction, and 
address key operational needs, including expanded in-district programming, literacy and early 
intervention, high school counseling, and technology security. 
Natick Public Schools remains a strong district with improving student outcomes, while continuing 
to serve a wide range of student needs. This budget reﬂects the resources required to maintain 
that work. 
We are grateful for the partnership with the T own and for the continued support of our schools. 
Thank you for your consideration.

Tax Levy
The Tax Levy continues to be the Town’s primary rev enue source, accounting for approximately 75% of
total General Fund revenues in FY 2027. As illustra ted in the historical revenue chart, the override,
combined with the annual Proposition 2½ increase of 2.5%  and New Growth, contributed to a step-
up in the levy beginning in FY 2026 and sustained g rowth into FY 2027.
For FY 2027, the total Tax Levy is budgeted at $162.99 million , reflecting the ongoing impact of the
override and continued growth in the levy limit. Th is revenue source provides the Town with a stable
and predictable funding base to support core munici pal and school services.
Local Receipts
Local Receipts represent the Town’s second-largest recurring revenue source, comprising
approximately 9.6% of total General Fund revenues i n FY 2027. Local Receipts experienced strong
growth in recent years, driven by motor vehicle exc ise, meals and hotel excise taxes and licenses and
permits.
For FY 2027, Local Receipts are budgeted at $21.65 million , reflecting a moderation from the strong
growth experienced in prior years. While economic c onditions remain uncertain, projections assume a
return to more sustainable growth rates, consistent  with long-term historical averages and the Town’s
conservative revenue forecasting practices.

Jon Marshall, Deputy Administrator of Operations, presented this Article 7, which is a cleanup
and rewrite of the existing bylaw on town revolving funds, Bylaw 41A. It does not create any 
new revolving funds or authorize any expenditures (companion Article 8 will authorize 
expenditures from the revolving funds). Review of existing Bylaw 41A was prompted by the 
establishment of the new stormwater revolving fund by fall 2025 town meeting. This rewrite is 
based on a model bylaw provided by the state Department of Revenue's Division of Municipal
Services (DLS). The primary changes are to add a Purpose section, to revise the description 
of the annual reporting on each revolving fund, and to move the description of the various 
funds and purposes into a table. In addition, the revision consistently identifies a department 
or board (rather than a particular officer of a department or board) as the entity authorized to 
spend each fund.
A FinCom member noted that the table includes a column for restrictions or conditions on 
expenses payable from each fund and that this column is empty. Mr. Marshall noted that 
several other communities’ revolving fund Bylaws were reviewed for restrictions and found to 
have none. Even so, the column (which exists in the DLS model bylaw) was retained in case 
the town were to add restrictions in the future. 
A FinCom member asked why the new Bylaw directs income from each fund to the general 
fund rather than be retained in the particular revolving fund. It was noted that this is a 
requirement of Massachusetts General Law Chapter 44,Section 53E½, the state law that 
permits towns to establish revolving funds. 
A FinCom member asked if the changes to the reporting section would mean that existing 
reporting on the revolving funds would no longer be available. Mr. Marshall indicated that the 
changes were semantic and not material - that no changes to the content of existing reports 
were intended.
FinCom voted unanimously to recommend that Town Meeting approve the motion for Article 7
as written.

FINANCE COMMITTEE PUBLIC HEARING AND DISCUSSION
The article was presented in the Finance Committee Hearing on March 12 by Bruce Evans of the 
Select Board and Chief James Hicks.  The police chief and the deputy chief are subject to mandatory 
retirement statutes this year.  For each of them to continue in his respective role into 2027 will require 
successful passage of enabling home rule petition legislation by the state legislature.  Article 12 is the 
article to enable the home rule petition for the chief’s continued employment.  It was stated that the 
average police force tenure is not historically high and that the supervisory experience on the force is 
generally light, especially with the imminent retirements of two lieutenants and one sergeant. It was 
stated that tight staffing currently for rank and file officers makes it difficult to promote officers needed 
for street duty.   Attempts to hire mid-career employees with more experience have so far not proven 
productive. Extending the chief’s tenure would let him see through the completion of a regional 
dispatch project currently underway, and would create time to develop succession options.
A Finance Committee member asked if other municipalities commonly extend contracts beyond the 
statutory retirement age.  Chief Hicks responded that two communities are known to have done so.
A Finance Committee member asked if alternative arrangements might be considered involving 
consulting or project-based engagement without an extended contract.  Mr. Evans indicated some 
openness to considering flexible or creative alternative options in parallel with pursuing the home rule 
petition. 
A Finance Committee member noted that the subject is being raised close to the deadline for the 
action it contemplates, when the facts of the chief’s age have been known and could have prompted 
action sooner.  
A Finance Committee member noted that among the points made in support when Town Meeting 
acted on the Deputy Chief position was that it could facilitate succession planning, and yet the current 
configuration does not facilitate succession without the home rule petitions.
The presenters responded that while the intention of the Deputy Chief position included succession 
planning, that intention depended on the applicant pool for the deputy position supporting the 
intention.  It was also noted that some of the recent or imminent retirements were unexpected.
A motion was made and seconded to recommend favorable action on the motion for article 12 as 
presented.  The vote was 6-4-0.  The affirmative vote did not reach the quantum of 8 votes required 
for a Finance Committee recommendation.

Joshua Ostroff presented Article 18. Mr. Ostroff presented this article as both a celebration of 
the 250th anniversary of the 1776 Natick Resolve for Independence, a Natick Town Meeting 
resolution supporting the then-pending declaration of independence from England, and a 
reaffirmation of our of current commitment to the United States Constitution. Further, the 
article intends to positively affirm the indigenous peoples of the land currently comprising the 
United States and Natick, to counter the derogatory and negative references to such peoples 
included in both the declaration of independence and the US Constitution.

--- Page 121 ---

This article was reviewed and discussed at two separate FinCom meetings. At the initial 
meeting, certain FinCom members pointed to language in an earlier version of the motion that
pointed to our current political climate in a way that such members felt would be divisive. 
Such language was removed from the version presented above, which was the subject of the 
second FinCom meeting at which this article was discussed.  
A FinCom member asked if the sponsor would be willing further amend the motion to remove 
language stating that Town Meeting “opposes the curtailment of rights embodied in the 
United States Constitution and its amendments that Town Meeting members have taken an 
oath to support and uphold” also on the basis of such language potentially being divisive. The 
sponsor declined. It was noted that although it was unanimously adopted the original 1776 
Natick Resolve for Independence was likely divisive. Further, other FinCom members 
expressed that the language in the current version of the motion “opposing tyranny and 
injustice” and “opposing the curtailment of rights embodied in the Constitution” ought to be 
non-controversial given that Town Meeting members are required to pledge allegiance to the 
Constitution as a condition to taking their elective positions.
A member of the public objected to the reference to ‘indigenous’ peoples and expressed 
preference for the generic phrase ‘all’ peoples. The sponsor declined to make such change. 
Another member of the public and a FinCom member each noted that both the Declaration of 
Independence and the US Constitution include disparaging references to indigenous peoples.
The sponsor noted that countering such disparaging references are specifically why Article 18
includes language naming and proclaiming respect for such indigenous peoples. 
Niki Lefebrvre, Ph.D., Executive Director of the Natick Historical Society (which has the 
original 1776 Natick Resolve for Independence in its collection and has the full text of the 
resolution available on its website) expressed enthusiastic support for Article 18. She noted 
that resolves for independence were requested by the Continental Congress and were 
passed by 58 towns in Massachusetts including Natick. As a 250-year-old document, she 
noted that the 1776 Natick Resolve for Independence, does include dated and challenging 
language and assumptions, and she expressed that Town Meeting debate about such 
language and assumptions would be a fitting tribute to the spirit of the resolve and its 
resonance in 2026. 
FinCom voted on a motion to amend the motion for Article 18 to remove the 5th bullet point. 
The motion failed, receiving only 2 favorable votes. 
FinCom then voted favorably to recommend that Town Meeting approve the motion for Article
18 as appearing above.
